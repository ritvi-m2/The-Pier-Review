The Engineering Behind Stealth Warships

By The Pier Review — February 23, 2026

How does one hide a 15,000-ton warship in a vast and open environment such as the high seas? In naval warfare, ‘stealth’ is not defined by visual concealment, but rather by reducing the Radar Cross Section (RCS).

The primary objective of maritime stealth is not to achieve literal invisibility, but to reflect a much smaller vessel, such as a fishing boat. This process is achieved by minimising the energy reflected to an enemy sensor. Engineers can reduce a destroyer’s detection range or, more crucially, shrink its electronic profile.

Geometry of Reducing Detection

A striking feature of these modern stealth vessels is their unconventional silhouette. Most traditional naval architecture has vertical bulkheads and 90-degree corner reflectors where a deck meets a part of the ship that rises above the main hull (a superstructure). These superstructures act as perfect retroreflectors: objects that cause light to bounce back where it came from.

Engineers have to utilise constructs such as ‘planform alignment’ and ‘faceted structures’ to counter this phenomenon. By sloping the hull from precise angles, incoming electromagnetic waves are deflected away from the enemy receiver. ‘Tumblehome’ hull, the area where the ship narrows above the waterline, is a perfect example. In addition, any external equipment (e.g. deck cranes, lifeboats) is shielded behind radar-opaque screens or even integrated into the structure to maintain a stealthy silhouette.

Materials Science

Radar-absorbent material (RAM) is applied to ‘hot spots’ where the geometry of the ship is insufficient for concealment. These coatings consist of magnetic ferrites (iron oxide compounds combined with metals) or carbon particles on a polymer matrix. When a radar wave strikes RAM, the alternating magnetic field of the wave induce oscillations within the coating. Through certain processes, the electromagnetic energy is converted into thermal energy (heat), which is then dissipated into the ship’s structure.

Furthermore, the use of ‘Frequency Selective Surfaces’ (FSS) hides the ship’s communication antennas to be hidden behind bulkheads that are transparent to friendly frequencies but absorbent to enemy radar signals.

Acoustic Stealth

As radar waves cannot penetrate the ocean’s surface, water acts as a natural shield that hides everything below the waterline from electronic detection. Therefore, acoustic signatures (a particular ship’s distinct combination of noises) are the primary means for an enemy to locate a vessel. ‘Acoustic quieting’ is the isolation of a ship’s mechanical vibration from its hull.

Naval architects use ‘double-raft mounting’, where engines are placed on vibration-dampening rubber mounts, for acoustic quieting. Furthermore, the Prairie-Masker system can be employed. This system essentially ejects small air bubbles from the hull and propeller blades, which change the density of the surrounding water, muffling the machinery noise and disrupting the cavitation hiss generated by the propellers. What this does is make the ship’s acoustic profile indistinguishable from typical ocean noise.

Thermal Masking

Most modern anti-ship missiles transition to infrared (IR) homing during their terminal phase ( the last few seconds before impacting the ship). A warship’s gas turbine often reaches temperatures exceeding 500°C, which creates a massive thermal contrast with the cold ocean background.

To tone down this effect, stealth ships use ‘Boundary Layer Infrared Suppression Systems’ (BLISS), which mix hot exhaust gases with large volumes of air before they are expelled. This significantly lowers the exhaust plume’s temperature. Additionally, active hull cooling (seawater sprayed over internal pipes) ensures the ship’s ‘skin’ temperature is in equilibrium with the surrounding environment, rendering it nearly invisible to thermal imaging sensors.

Conclusion

The engineering of stealth warships are masterclasses in concealment. Each angled plate and RAM coating adds weight and cost. In an era of long-range precision munitions, the ability to remain concealed is the best defence. By combining spectrums of electromagnetic radiation, material science and thermodynamics, naval architects ensure that a 15,000-ton warship may stay hidden.
